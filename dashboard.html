What you are looking at. 5000 computer-generated propeller designs for 3-D printing, each simulated in free vertical flight: the propeller is spun up to a chosen launch RPM, released, and climbs on its own thrust while its spin decays. The key result per design is h max — the peak height it reaches. 100 designs were printed and 30 were flight-tested to validate the simulation.

The Launch RPM selector re-draws the highlighted charts at that release speed. Geometry charts don't change — the designs stay the same, only how fast they're launched.

Glossary of the terms used on this page
h max (aero)
peak climb height from the pure aerodynamic simulation — an idealised release.
h max (corrected)
the same flight after the calibrated launcher correction: the real test rig strips most of the spin as it releases the propeller, so real heights are much lower. This is what the launcher actually achieves and what measurements are compared against.
T/W
thrust-to-weight ratio at release. Below 1 (red line) the design cannot lift off.
static thrust / torque
thrust and shaft torque at the launch RPM at zero climb speed, from the QProp blade-element model.
FOM
figure of merit — hover efficiency (ideal power / actual power); higher is more efficient. Independent of launch RPM.
Izz
spin-axis moment of inertia; sets how fast the spin decays after release.
confidence score
quality of the airfoil aerodynamic data behind a design's simulation (1 = all blade sections solved directly, lower = fallbacks were needed).
radial evolution
how chord (blade width), twist and thickness vary from hub to tip; each faint line is one sampled design, the black line is the average.

The representative subset. Printing all 5000 designs is impossible, so 100 were chosen to cover the design space as evenly as possible (greedy k-centre selection). These charts check that coverage: grey dots are all 5000 designs projected onto their main axes of geometric variation (principal components), red dots are the 100 printed ones, green diamonds the 30 flight-tested ones. Good coverage = red dots spread across the grey cloud rather than clumped.
